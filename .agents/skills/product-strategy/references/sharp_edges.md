# Product Strategy - Sharp Edges

## Build It They Will Come

### **Id**
build-it-they-will-come
### **Summary**
Building without validating the problem exists
### **Severity**
critical
### **Situation**
  Brilliant idea. Elegant technology. 6 months building in stealth mode.
  Launch to crickets. Nobody cares. Problem wasn't validated.
  
### **Why**
  Founders fall in love with solutions before validating problems.
  Assume their own pain is universal. Build in isolation fearing
  someone will "steal the idea." Confuse technical innovation with market need.
  
### **Solution**
  # Before writing a line of code:
  Talk to 50+ potential customers
  
  # Use the Mom Test:
  Ask about their life, not your idea
  "Tell me about the last time you..."
  Not: "Would you use an app that..."
  
  # Look for existing workarounds:
  Spreadsheets, manual processes, duct-tape solutions
  If people aren't trying to solve this with bad solutions,
  they won't pay for your good one
  
  # Validation artifacts:
  - 50+ customer interview notes
  - Pattern documentation
  - Willingness to pay signals
  
### **Symptoms**
  - "We'll validate after we build the MVP"
  - "The product will sell itself"
  - "We're in stealth mode"
  - No customer interviews documented
### **Detection Pattern**


## Feature Factory Trap

### **Id**
feature-factory-trap
### **Summary**
Measuring success by features shipped instead of outcomes achieved
### **Severity**
critical
### **Situation**
  Roadmap is a list of features. Success = shipping. Customers request
  features and you build them. Product becomes bloated, unfocused,
  indistinguishable from competitors.
  
### **Why**
  Features are easy to measure. Sales brings feature requests. Competitors
  ship features. Nobody asks "what outcome does this drive?"
  Building feels like progress.
  
### **Solution**
  # Shift from output to outcome:
  Every feature must tie to:
  - Measurable user outcome
  - Business metric
  
  # The 10x test:
  "Will this make the product 10x better at its core job?"
  If no, probably shouldn't build
  
  # Say no to 90% of feature requests:
  Focused product doing one thing brilliantly
  > Bloated product doing everything poorly
  
  # PRD requirements:
  - What outcome does this drive?
  - How will we measure success?
  - What will we NOT build to build this?
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Roadmap is feature list without outcomes
  - "Competitors have X, we need X"
  - Success measured in features shipped
  - PRDs describe what, not why
### **Detection Pattern**


## More Data Procrastination

### **Id**
more-data-procrastination
### **Summary**
Endless research instead of shipping and learning
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  Survey after survey. 100 customer interviews. Market analysis.
  Elaborate spreadsheets. Never ship anything because "still validating."
  
### **Why**
  Research feels like progress without risk of failure. Always one more
  question. Analysis paralysis disguises itself as rigor. Fear of being
  wrong masquerades as thoroughness.
  
### **Solution**
  # Set hard deadline for decision-making:
  After 20-30 interviews, you have enough signal
  
  # Use "Disagree and Commit":
  Make a decision even with imperfect info
  Ship, learn, iterate
  
  # Build the smallest thing:
  Test your riskiest assumption
  Real learning comes from market contact
  
  # Research time-box:
  4-6 weeks max for validation phase
  Then ship something
  
### **Symptoms**
  - "We need more data before deciding"
  - Validation phase extending beyond 4-6 weeks
  - Multiple pivots based on research alone
  - Perfect information seeking
### **Detection Pattern**


## Were Different Blindness

### **Id**
were-different-blindness
### **Summary**
Dismissing competitors because you believe you're unique
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  Dismiss competitors because "we're different." Believe your approach
  is unique. Meanwhile, customers see you as yet another option in
  a crowded market.
  
### **Why**
  Founders too close to see from customer's perspective. Technical
  differences feel significant internally but invisible externally.
  Optimism bias underweights competitive threats.
  
### **Solution**
  # Do the Screenshot Test:
  Put your landing page next to 5 competitors
  If customer can't immediately see why you're different,
  you're not.
  
  # 5-second rule:
  Differentiation must be obvious in 5 seconds
  Not explained in 5 minutes
  
  # Define or be defined:
  Own a category
  Or get lumped into existing one
  
  # Position on outcomes, not features:
  Features are copyable
  Outcomes and stories are not
  
### **Symptoms**
  - "Our competitors don't really compete with us"
  - Differentiation requires explanation
  - Feature comparison shows similarity
  - Positioning by features, not outcomes
### **Detection Pattern**


## Premature Scaling Death Spiral

### **Id**
premature-scaling-death-spiral
### **Summary**
Scaling before product-market fit is confirmed
### **Severity**
critical
### **Situation**
  Early traction - few paying customers, some press, maybe funding.
  Hire aggressively, build out product, scale marketing.
  Then realize: didn't have PMF. Had early adopter curiosity.
  
### **Why**
  Pressure from investors. Early success creates false confidence.
  Hiring feels like progress. Difference between "some people want this"
  and "the market wants this" is subtle but fatal.
  
### **Solution**
  # Define PMF metrics BEFORE scaling:
  - 40%+ would be "very disappointed" without product
  - Organic word-of-mouth growth
  - Low churn (<5% monthly B2B, <10% B2C)
  - Customers expanding usage unprompted
  
  # Don't scale until these metrics are hit:
  Growth before PMF = pouring water into leaky bucket
  
  # PMF signals:
  Retention curves flatten
  Users pulling product (not you pushing)
  Referrals happening organically
  Usage increasing over time
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Scaling before clear retention metrics
  - High CAC with unclear LTV
  - "Growth will solve our problems"
  - Churn >5% monthly B2B, >10% B2C
### **Detection Pattern**


## Everyone Is Our Customer

### **Id**
everyone-is-our-customer
### **Summary**
Targeting too broad an audience and serving no one well
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  Target market is "anyone who needs X." Afraid to narrow.
  Messaging is generic. Product tries to serve everyone and delights no one.
  
### **Why**
  Narrowing feels like leaving money on the table. Investors want big TAM.
  "Small" markets feel limiting. Optimize for not missing anyone rather than
  deeply serving someone.
  
### **Solution**
  # Start with smallest viable market:
  Market that can sustain your business
  And you can dominate
  
  # Geoffrey Moore targeting:
  Specific person + Specific situation + Specific problem
  
  # Be the only solution for someone:
  Rather than an option for everyone
  You can expand later
  You can't un-dilute focus
  
  # ICP definition:
  Not: "Businesses that need project management"
  But: "15-50 person agencies struggling with client communication,
        using 3+ tools, losing $10K+/month to inefficiency"
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Target market described as broad demographic
  - "Our TAM is $X billion"
  - Messaging uses generic value props
  - Features designed for "users" not specific personas
### **Detection Pattern**


## Solution First Backwards Build

### **Id**
solution-first-backwards-build
### **Summary**
Starting with technology or solution instead of problem
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  Start with "We'll use AI to..." or "An app that lets you..."
  Retrofit problems to justify solution. Problem you solve isn't
  the problem customers care about.
  
### **Why**
  Solutions are exciting; problems are boring. Technology capabilities
  inspire ideas. "Build something cool" is more fun than "understand deep pain."
  
### **Solution**
  # Force yourself to articulate problem without solution:
  Can you describe the problem without mentioning what you build?
  If not, you don't understand it
  
  # Use the 5 Whys:
  Get to root cause
  Surface problem often isn't real problem
  
  # Interview about workflow:
  Don't mention what you're building
  "Tell me about how you currently..."
  "What's frustrating about that?"
  
  # Problem statement test:
  If you can't describe it in their words,
  you don't understand it
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Pitch starts with solution or technology
  - Problem statement uses your terminology
  - Customers don't recognize problem description
  - Features before use cases
### **Detection Pattern**


## Feedback Whiplash

### **Id**
feedback-whiplash
### **Summary**
Changing direction based on every piece of feedback
### **Severity**
medium
### **Situation**
  Every customer interview sends you in new direction. Negative feedback
  triggers pivots. Oscillate between strategies based on last conversation.
  Product becomes Frankenstein of different visions.
  
### **Why**
  Individual feedback feels compelling in the moment. Want to be responsive.
  Pattern recognition across feedback is hard. Confirmation bias makes us
  overweight feedback matching our fears.
  
### **Solution**
  # Collect feedback in batches:
  Don't act on single conversations
  Wait for patterns across 10+
  
  # Distinguish feedback types:
  Execution feedback: How we built it
  Strategy feedback: What we're building
  Treat differently
  
  # Create a feedback parking lot:
  Log it, don't act
  Review patterns weekly/monthly
  
  # One opinion = anecdote
  # Ten opinions with pattern = data
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Strategy changes after single conversations
  - Product direction unclear to team
  - Multiple pivots in short timeframe
  - Acting on outlier opinions
### **Detection Pattern**


## Vanity Metrics Mirage

### **Id**
vanity-metrics-mirage
### **Summary**
Celebrating metrics that don't indicate business health
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  Dashboard shows growing users, downloads, pageviews, followers.
  Celebrate these wins. But revenue is flat, retention is poor,
  engagement is shallow.
  
### **Why**
  Vanity metrics go up and to the right - they feel good. Real metrics
  are harder and often embarrassing. We report what looks good,
  not what matters.
  
### **Solution**
  # Define your OMTM (One Metric That Matters):
  The single number indicating product health
  
  # For most products:
  Weekly active users who complete core action
  OR monthly retention rate
  OR revenue per user
  
  # Key question:
  "If users aren't coming back unprompted and doing
   the core thing repeatedly, nothing else matters."
  
  # Metric depth:
  Not just "opens" but "completes"
  Not just "signups" but "activated"
  Not just "users" but "weekly actives"
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Celebrating signups without activation
  - No cohort retention analysis
  - Engagement metrics without depth
  - "Users" counted without defining active
### **Detection Pattern**


## V2 Will Fix It

### **Id**
v2-will-fix-it
### **Summary**
Deferring core issues to a future version that never comes
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  Early users complain about core issues. Acknowledge but say
  "we'll fix it in V2." Keep building new features instead of
  fixing fundamentals. V2 never comes.
  
### **Why**
  New features more exciting than fixing old problems. Backlog grows
  faster than capacity. Core issues feel expensive. Always pressure
  to ship something new.
  
### **Solution**
  # If something is broken in core flow:
  Stop everything until it's fixed
  
  # Technical and UX debt compound:
  Like financial debt
  Interest accumulates
  
  # Quality rule:
  A product that does one thing flawlessly
  > A product with ten features that all kind of work
  
  # "V2 will fix it" is the biggest lie in product:
  V2 doesn't come
  Or V2 has its own issues
  Fix it now
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Known issues in core flow unfixed for months
  - "We'll fix it later" in sprint notes
  - New features shipping while fundamentals broken
  - User complaints about same issues over time
### **Detection Pattern**


## Copycat Deathmatch

### **Id**
copycat-deathmatch
### **Summary**
Building "X but better" and entering features arms race
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  See successful product and build "better" version. Copy features,
  add your twist. Enter crowded market hoping to out-execute.
  Find yourself in features arms race with no differentiation.
  
### **Why**
  Validated markets feel safer. "X but better" is easy pitch. Copying
  reduces research burden. Underestimate incumbent advantages:
  brand, distribution, network effects.
  
### **Solution**
  # Compete on dimension incumbents can't or won't:
  Find the underserved segment they ignore
  Change the game entirely
  
  # Ask:
  "What can I be the only one doing?"
  Not: "What can I do better?"
  
  # Blue ocean strategy:
  Different is better than better
  New categories > old categories
  Serve who they don't serve
  
  # If you must compete directly:
  10x better on dimension that matters
  Not 10% better on everything
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Product described as "X but better"
  - Feature roadmap mirrors competitor
  - Competing on price or features alone
  - Incumbent could easily copy differentiation
### **Detection Pattern**


## Users Dont Know What They Want

### **Id**
users-dont-know-what-they-want
### **Summary**
Ignoring feedback by misquoting Jobs/Ford
### **Severity**
medium
### **Situation**
  Invoke "users don't know what they want" to justify ignoring feedback.
  Believe your vision is superior to user input. Build what you think
  they need, not what they demonstrate they need.
  
### **Why**
  The quote is misunderstood. Easier to trust instinct than synthesize
  feedback. Some founders have been right ignoring feedback (survivorship bias).
  
### **Solution**
  # The actual insight:
  Users don't know what SOLUTION they want
  They DO know what PROBLEMS they have
  
  # Your job:
  Understand the problem deeply enough
  To invent the right solution
  
  # Jobs spent thousands of hours:
  Understanding user behavior
  BEFORE inventing
  The iPod came from understanding people's relationship with music
  
  # Listen for:
  Problems (yes)
  Behaviors (yes)
  Solutions (interpret, don't implement literally)
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Dismissing feedback as "users don't get it"
  - No customer behavior observation
  - Building based on internal intuition alone
  - Surprise when users don't adopt features
### **Detection Pattern**
