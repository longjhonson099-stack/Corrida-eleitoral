# Yc Playbook - Sharp Edges

## Building Before Talking

### **Id**
building-before-talking
### **Summary**
Building features before talking to users
### **Severity**
critical
### **Situation**
  3 weeks into YC batch. Heads down coding. Haven't talked to a single
  user this week. "We'll get feedback after we ship this feature."
  
### **Why**
  Every week in YC is precious. Building in isolation means building the wrong
  thing. By the time you ship, you've lost 3 weeks that could have been
  learning. YC companies that talk to users weekly grow 5x faster.
  
### **Solution**
  # Weekly user contact minimum:
  - 10+ user conversations per week (not surveys, conversations)
  - Schedule them on Monday, protect that time
  
  # The YC user interview cadence:
  Monday: Schedule 10-15 calls for the week
  Tue-Thu: Talk to users
  Friday: Ship based on learnings
  
  # What to ask:
  - "What's the hardest part about [problem]?"
  - "Tell me about the last time you..."
  - "How are you solving this today?"
  - Never: "Would you use this feature?"
  
### **Symptoms**
  - We'll talk to users after we ship
  - Users don't know what they want
  - We're building what we know they need
  - Zero user calls on calendar this week
### **Detection Pattern**


## Demo Day Distraction

### **Id**
demo-day-distraction
### **Summary**
Optimizing for demo day instead of building a real business
### **Severity**
critical
### **Situation**
  6 weeks before demo day. All focus shifts to pitch, deck, and vanity
  metrics that look good on stage. Product development slows. User
  conversations stop. "We need to focus on fundraising."
  
### **Why**
  Demo day is 2 minutes. Your company is (hopefully) 10+ years. Founders who
  optimize for demo day often raise money but fail afterwards because they
  stopped building. The best demo day pitches come from real traction.
  
### **Solution**
  # Demo day is a side effect, not the goal:
  Keep building and talking to users through demo day prep
  
  # The 80/20 rule:
  80% time on product/users
  20% time on pitch prep
  
  # Best pitch = real traction:
  "We have X users doing Y" beats any polished narrative
  
  # After demo day matters more:
  The companies that keep building during fundraising
  are the ones worth funding
  
### **Symptoms**
  - We need to pause product for demo day prep
  - Hours spent on pitch animations
  - No user calls in last 2 weeks
  - Let's make our metrics look better
### **Detection Pattern**


## Vanity Metrics Theater

### **Id**
vanity-metrics-theater
### **Summary**
Reporting metrics that look good but don't matter
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  Investor update: "10,000 signups! 50,000 page views! Featured in
  TechCrunch!" Reality: 50 active users, $500 MRR, 90% churn.
  
### **Why**
  Vanity metrics feel good but hide the truth. Investors see through them.
  Partners definitely see through them. You're only fooling yourself, and
  that's the most dangerous person to fool.
  
### **Solution**
  # The only metrics that matter for YC:
  - Revenue (or strong proxy)
  - Active users (weekly, not monthly)
  - Retention/churn
  - Growth rate (week over week)
  
  # Format for investor updates:
  Revenue: $X (+Y% WoW)
  Active Users: X (+Y% WoW)
  Key Insight: [one learning from users]
  Biggest Challenge: [be honest]
  Ask: [specific help needed]
  
  # The "revenue or engagement" test:
  If it doesn't drive revenue or engagement,
  it's a vanity metric
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Leading with press mentions
  - "Downloads" instead of "active users"
  - "Signups" instead of "retention"
  - No revenue number in update
### **Detection Pattern**


## Office Hours Theater

### **Id**
office-hours-theater
### **Summary**
Telling partners what they want to hear instead of truth
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  Office hours. Partner asks hard questions. Founder spins positive.
  "Users love it!" (3 users). "Growth is strong!" (friends signing up).
  Partner can't help because they don't know real situation.
  
### **Why**
  Partners have seen 4000+ companies. They can help with any problem. But
  they can only help if they know the truth. Spin wastes everyone's time
  and deprives you of the best advice available.
  
### **Solution**
  # Come with real problems:
  "We're struggling with X, we've tried Y and Z"
  
  # Be specific:
  Not: "Users are confused"
  Yes: "3 of 5 users couldn't complete checkout, here's where they dropped"
  
  # Partners want to help:
  They've seen every problem, probably have solutions
  But only if you're honest
  
  # The vulnerability advantage:
  Founders who are honest about struggles
  get better advice and more partner support
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Only sharing good news
  - Vague answers to hard questions
  - We're figuring it out
  - Not asking for specific help
### **Detection Pattern**


## Pivot Without Conviction

### **Id**
pivot-without-conviction
### **Summary**
Pivoting because things are hard, not because you learned something
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  Week 4. Initial idea is hard. User acquisition is slow. "Maybe we should
  pivot to B2B." Week 6. B2B is hard. "Maybe we should try a different
  vertical." Endless pivoting, never committing.
  
### **Why**
  Every good idea feels impossible at first. Pivoting is sometimes right,
  but pivoting because things are hard is always wrong. The best companies
  look like bad ideas initially.
  
### **Solution**
  # Pivot criteria (must have 2+ to pivot):
  - Talked to 50+ users about this problem
  - Clear pattern: people don't have this problem
  - Found a better problem during research
  - Been at it for 3+ months with no signal
  
  # Don't pivot because:
  - Growth is slow (it always is at first)
  - It's hard (it always is)
  - Someone said it won't work (they always do)
  
  # Commit fully:
  Pick a direction and go hard for 6-8 weeks
  Then evaluate with real data, not feelings
  
### **Symptoms**
  - New idea every office hours
  - We're exploring options
  - Less than 3 weeks on current idea
  - Pivoting without user research
### **Detection Pattern**


## Launch Procrastination

### **Id**
launch-procrastination
### **Summary**
Waiting until it's "ready" to launch
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  Week 6. "We'll launch next week when we add this feature."
  Week 8. "Almost ready, just need to fix these bugs."
  Week 10. Still haven't launched. Demo day in 2 weeks.
  
### **Why**
  Launching is scary because it's the moment of truth. But every day you don't
  launch is a day you're not learning from real users. The market doesn't care
  about your feelings. Launch.
  
### **Solution**
  # The YC launch rule:
  If you're not embarrassed by v1, you launched too late
  
  # Launch checklist (that's it):
  - Core value prop works (barely)
  - Users can sign up
  - You can contact users who sign up
  
  # What doesn't matter for launch:
  - Perfect UI
  - All features
  - Scalability
  - Edge cases
  
  # Do it now:
  Pick a date this week
  Tell someone so you're accountable
  Ship
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Just one more feature
  - We need to polish the UI
  - What if it breaks?
  - In batch 4+ weeks without launching
### **Detection Pattern**


## Yc Network Ignorance

### **Id**
yc-network-ignorance
### **Summary**
Not leveraging the YC network and alumni
### **Severity**
medium
### **Situation**
  Struggling with a problem. Don't know any other YC founders. Haven't
  posted in Bookface. Haven't reached out to alumni in your space.
  Trying to figure everything out alone.
  
### **Why**
  The YC network is worth more than the money. Alumni have solved your
  exact problem. Batch-mates are going through the same struggles. Not
  using the network is leaving millions of dollars of value on the table.
  
### **Solution**
  # Week 1 actions:
  - Post intro in Bookface
  - Find 10 alumni in your space, message them
  - Find 5 batch-mates with similar challenges
  
  # When stuck:
  - Post in Bookface asking for help
  - Message 3-5 alumni who've solved this
  - Ask partners for intro to relevant founders
  
  # Pay it forward:
  - Help other founders when you can
  - The network compounds over time
  
### **Symptoms**
  - I don't want to bother anyone
  - Never posted in Bookface
  - Don't know batch-mates
  - Solving everything alone
### **Detection Pattern**


## Cofounder Conflict Denial

### **Id**
cofounder-conflict-denial
### **Summary**
Ignoring cofounder tensions until they explode
### **Severity**
critical
### **Situation**
  Growing tension with cofounder. Different visions. Unequal workload.
  Communication breaking down. "We'll work it out." Blow up at demo day.
  
### **Why**
  Cofounder breakups are the #1 killer of early-stage startups. They rarely
  happen suddenly - there are always warning signs. Ignoring them doesn't
  make them go away.
  
### **Solution**
  # Weekly cofounder check-in:
  - What's working?
  - What's not working?
  - Any resentments building?
  
  # When things feel off:
  - Talk about it immediately, not later
  - Consider bringing in partner as mediator
  - Better to address early than let it fester
  
  # Hard conversations:
  - Role clarity: who owns what?
  - Equity: is it still fair?
  - Vision: are we aligned on the goal?
  
  # If it's not working:
  Better to split early than blow up later
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Avoiding conversations
  - We'll figure it out later
  - Different stories to partners
  - Not talking outside of work stuff
### **Detection Pattern**


## Post Batch Cliff

### **Id**
post-batch-cliff
### **Summary**
Losing momentum after YC ends
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  Demo day done. Raised a round. Batch ends. Suddenly no structure.
  No weekly office hours. No batch-mates pushing you. Momentum dies.
  
### **Why**
  YC provides external accountability and pressure. When it ends, founders
  often unconsciously relax. The companies that win are the ones that
  maintain YC intensity indefinitely.
  
### **Solution**
  # Keep the cadence:
  - Weekly metrics tracking (don't stop)
  - Monthly investor updates
  - Regular user conversations
  
  # Create accountability:
  - Mastermind group with batch-mates
  - Monthly check-ins with partner
  - Public goals and commitments
  
  # Maintain intensity:
  - YC was the warm-up
  - The real work starts now
  - Keep the urgency
  
### **Symptoms**
  - We'll take a break after fundraising
  - Stopped tracking weekly metrics
  - No investor updates in 2+ months
  - Lost touch with batch-mates
### **Detection Pattern**


## Investor Update Neglect

### **Id**
investor-update-neglect
### **Summary**
Not sending regular investor updates
### **Severity**
medium
### **Situation**
  Raised money. Got busy building. Haven't updated investors in 3 months.
  When you need help or more money, no one knows what you're doing.
  
### **Why**
  Investor updates aren't just obligation - they're leverage. Regular updates
  keep you top of mind for intros, follow-on, and help. Dark founders get
  forgotten and miss opportunities.
  
### **Solution**
  # Monthly update template:
  
  ## Wins
  - [2-3 bullet points]
  
  ## Metrics
  - Revenue: $X (+Y% MoM)
  - Users: X (+Y% MoM)
  - Key metric: X
  
  ## Learnings
  - [What you learned this month]
  
  ## Challenges
  - [Be honest about struggles]
  
  ## Asks
  - [Specific help needed]
  
  # Best practices:
  - Same day each month
  - 5 minute read max
  - Be honest about bad news
  - Specific asks get responses
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Last update 2+ months ago
  - Only update when need something
  - Hide bad news
  - No specific asks
### **Detection Pattern**


## Feature Request Following

### **Id**
feature-request-following
### **Summary**
Building what users ask for instead of what they need
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  Users asking for features. Building all of them. Product becomes Frankenstein.
  Core value prop diluted. "But users asked for it!"
  
### **Why**
  Users are experts on their problems, not solutions. When they ask for features,
  they're usually describing symptoms, not root causes. Building requests creates
  bloat. Understanding problems creates breakthroughs.
  
### **Solution**
  # When user requests a feature:
  "What are you trying to accomplish?"
  "Why is that important?"
  "How are you doing this today?"
  
  # The 5 Whys:
  Keep asking why until you find root cause
  Then solve that, not the surface request
  
  # Say no by default:
  Unless 5+ users have same underlying problem
  And it aligns with core value prop
  Don't build it
  
  # Jobs to be Done:
  What job is user hiring your product to do?
  Features that don't serve that job dilute the product
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Building every requested feature
  - Roadmap is user request list
  - Product scope keeps expanding
  - Lost sight of core value prop
### **Detection Pattern**


## Tarpit Idea Trap

### **Id**
tarpit-idea-trap
### **Summary**
Pursuing an idea that sounds good but has structural problems
### **Severity**
critical
### **Situation**
  Consumer social app. Hardware for X. Marketplace for Y. Idea sounds great
  in pitch but has fundamental challenges that make success nearly impossible.
  
### **Why**
  Some ideas are tarpits - they attract founders but have structural problems
  that make them nearly impossible. Consumer social needs viral growth. Hardware
  needs capital and manufacturing. Marketplaces need both sides simultaneously.
  
### **Solution**
  # Common tarpits to avoid (or approach with eyes open):
  - Consumer social without viral mechanic
  - Hardware without $10M+ for manufacturing
  - Two-sided marketplace without chicken-egg solution
  - Regulated industries without domain expertise
  - Enterprise sales without connections
  
  # Questions to ask:
  - Why hasn't this been solved?
  - What's my unfair advantage?
  - What do I know that others don't?
  
  # If you're in a tarpit:
  - Find the niche within the niche
  - Or get out early before you sink
  
### **Symptoms**
  - It's a social network for X
  - We're building hardware
  - It's a marketplace
  - No clear unfair advantage
### **Detection Pattern**
