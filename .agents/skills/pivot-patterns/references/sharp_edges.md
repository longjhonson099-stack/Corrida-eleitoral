# Pivot Patterns - Sharp Edges

## Pivot From Frustration

### **Id**
pivot-from-frustration
### **Summary**
Pivoting because things are hard, not because of learning
### **Severity**
critical
### **Situation**
  Week 4. Initial idea is hard. User acquisition slow. Lets pivot.
  Week 8. New idea is also hard. Pivot again. Endless thrashing,
  never going deep enough to learn.
  
### **Why**
  Every good idea feels impossible at first. Hard is normal. Pivoting
  should be based on learning that the problem is not real, not based
  on difficulty of execution.
  
### **Solution**
  Pivot criteria (need multiple):
  
  1. Talked to 50+ users about this specific problem
  2. Clear pattern that problem does not exist or is not painful
  3. Discovered a clearly better problem during research
  4. Been at it 3+ months with no signal despite real effort
  
  Pivoting from difficulty is wrong. Pivoting from learning is right.
  
### **Symptoms**
  - New direction every few weeks
  - Less than 6 weeks on current idea
  - Frustration driving decisions
  - No user research behind pivot
### **Detection Pattern**


## Ignoring Pivot Signals

### **Id**
ignoring-pivot-signals
### **Summary**
Persisting despite clear evidence it is not working
### **Severity**
critical
### **Situation**
  200 user interviews. Nobody cares about problem. Still building.
  Investors concerned. Team confused. But we are committed to the
  vision. Down with the ship.
  
### **Why**
  Persistence is a virtue, but ignoring evidence is not. When data
  clearly shows the problem is not real, continuing is not grit,
  it is denial. Learning requires updating beliefs.
  
### **Solution**
  Define pivot triggers upfront:
  
  What evidence would make us reconsider?
  - X interviews with no interest
  - Y months with no paying customers
  - Z metric not achieved by date
  
  When trigger hits, evaluate honestly.
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Clear negative signals being ignored
  - Rationalizing away bad data
  - Team afraid to voice concerns
  - Hope as strategy
### **Detection Pattern**


## Stealth Pivot

### **Id**
stealth-pivot
### **Summary**
Pivoting without communicating to stakeholders
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  Building something different than what investors funded. Hoping
  nobody notices. Eventually someone does. Trust destroyed. Board
  angry. Team confused.
  
### **Why**
  Pivots are normal. Hiding them is not. Investors back teams, not
  just ideas. They expect pivots. They do not expect deception.
  Proactive communication builds trust.
  
### **Solution**
  Proactive pivot communication:
  
  To investors:
  1. Share what you learned (data, not just feelings)
  2. Explain why new direction is better
  3. What transfers from old direction
  4. New milestones and timeline
  5. Ask for their input
  
  Do this before they find out another way.
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Investors surprised by new direction
  - Secret building of new product
  - Avoiding board conversations
  - Different story to different stakeholders
### **Detection Pattern**


## Everything Pivot

### **Id**
everything-pivot
### **Summary**
Changing everything at once during a pivot
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  Pivoting to new market, new product, new business model, new team.
  Actually a complete restart. Nothing learned. Same mistakes likely.
  
### **Why**
  Good pivots preserve something valuable: team, technology, insight,
  customers. Changing everything means learning nothing. Find what
  to keep.
  
### **Solution**
  Preserve something:
  
  What can we keep?
  - Team and culture
  - Technology and codebase
  - Customer relationships
  - Domain expertise
  - Insight about the problem
  
  Change one thing, not everything. Learn from what is preserved.
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Nothing preserved from previous direction
  - Complete restart mentality
  - All new everything
  - Previous work completely discarded
### **Detection Pattern**


## Slow Pivot Execution

### **Id**
slow-pivot-execution
### **Summary**
Taking too long to execute once pivot decision is made
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  Decided to pivot three months ago. Still debating details. Old
  product still half-supported. New product half-built. Runway
  burning on both.
  
### **Why**
  Pivots require conviction and speed. Analysis paralysis during
  pivots burns runway without learning. Commit fully. Execute fast.
  Learn from the new direction quickly.
  
### **Solution**
  Fast pivot execution:
  
  Week 1: Decision finalized, communicated
  Week 2: Old product sunset plan, new product MVP defined
  Week 4: First version of new direction live
  
  Debate before decision. Speed after decision.
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Months between decision and execution
  - Old and new both half-maintained
  - Team unsure which direction to focus
  - Endless refinement of pivot plan
### **Detection Pattern**


## Pivot Blame

### **Id**
pivot-blame
### **Summary**
Blaming market or idea instead of learning from pivot
### **Severity**
medium
### **Situation**
  Previous idea failed. Explanation: The market was not ready. Users
  did not get it. Competition was unfair. No examination of what we
  got wrong.
  
### **Why**
  Every failure has lessons. Blaming external factors prevents learning.
  What did we misjudge? What would we do differently? Honest reflection
  enables better next attempt.
  
### **Solution**
  Pivot retrospective:
  
  What did we believe that was wrong?
  What signals did we miss?
  What would we do differently?
  What did we learn about ourselves as a team?
  
  Document learnings before starting new direction.
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Blame on market or competition
  - No self-reflection
  - Same mistakes in new direction
  - External attribution for failure
### **Detection Pattern**


## Half Pivot

### **Id**
half-pivot
### **Summary**
Pivoting strategy but not fully committing resources
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  Officially pivoted to B2B. But still maintaining consumer product
  just in case. Team split. Neither gets full attention. Both fail.
  
### **Why**
  Pivots require full commitment. Hedging splits resources and
  attention. Neither direction gets what it needs. Pick one and
  commit fully.
  
### **Solution**
  Full commitment:
  
  1. Sunset old direction completely (with timeline)
  2. Move all resources to new direction
  3. Communicate the change clearly
  4. Burn bridges if needed for focus
  
  If you are not willing to go all in, do not pivot.
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Maintaining old product just in case
  - Resources split between directions
  - Hedging language
  - Team unclear on priority
### **Detection Pattern**


## Wrong People Pivot

### **Id**
wrong-people-pivot
### **Summary**
Keeping team misaligned with new direction
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  Pivoted from consumer to enterprise. Team hired for consumer.
  Skill mismatch. Culture mismatch. Slow execution. Frustration
  on all sides.
  
### **Why**
  Different directions need different skills. Consumer and enterprise
  are different worlds. Keeping wrong people hoping they adapt usually
  fails. Honest assessment of fit required.
  
### **Solution**
  Team alignment assessment:
  
  For each team member:
  1. Are their skills aligned with new direction?
  2. Are they excited about new direction?
  3. Can they adapt, and do they want to?
  
  Have honest conversations early. Better for everyone to acknowledge
  misalignment than pretend it does not exist.
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Skill gaps in new direction
  - Team unenthusiastic about pivot
  - Slow execution in new direction
  - Culture clash with new market
### **Detection Pattern**


## No New Milestones

### **Id**
no-new-milestones
### **Summary**
Pivoting without defining new success criteria
### **Severity**
medium
### **Situation**
  Pivoted to new direction. Working hard. Unclear if it is working.
  No defined milestones. Months pass. Are we succeeding? Nobody knows.
  
### **Why**
  Pivots need clear success criteria. What would prove this direction
  is right? Without milestones, you cannot evaluate progress or decide
  if another pivot is needed.
  
### **Solution**
  New direction milestones:
  
  30 days: X user conversations completed
  60 days: Y first users acquired
  90 days: Z metric achieved
  
  Define what success looks like. Evaluate honestly at each checkpoint.
  
### **Symptoms**
  - No success metrics for new direction
  - Unclear if pivot is working
  - Months without clear evaluation
  - Cannot tell if another pivot needed
### **Detection Pattern**


## Investor Surprise

### **Id**
investor-surprise
### **Summary**
Investors learn about pivot after the fact
### **Severity**
medium
### **Situation**
  Pivoted without telling investors. Months later they find out.
  Feel deceived. Trust damaged. Future fundraising harder.
  
### **Why**
  Investors expect pivots. They do not expect surprises. Early
  communication builds trust. Shows you are thoughtful and in control.
  Surprises suggest hiding problems.
  
### **Solution**
  Proactive investor update:
  
  When considering pivot:
  1. Share the data driving the consideration
  2. Explain options you are evaluating
  3. Ask for input if appropriate
  
  When deciding to pivot:
  1. Communicate within a week of decision
  2. Share learning story
  3. Express confidence in new direction
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Investors learn from others
  - Delayed pivot communication
  - Surprised or angry investors
  - Trust issues with board
### **Detection Pattern**
