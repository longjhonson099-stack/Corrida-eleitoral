# Thought Leadership - Sharp Edges

## Claiming Not Earning

### **Id**
claiming-not-earning
### **Summary**
Calling yourself a thought leader
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
Self-proclaimed thought leader without recognition
### **Why**
  Thought leadership is granted, not claimed.
  Self-proclamation invites skepticism.
  Credibility must be earned.
  
### **Solution**
  ## Earning Thought Leadership
  
  ### Signs of Earned Authority
  
  **Others Say It**
  - Introduced as expert by others
  - Cited in industry content
  - Asked for quotes/opinions
  - Invited to speak (not applied)
  
  **Evidence of Impact**
  - Content widely shared
  - Frameworks adopted by others
  - Ideas enter industry vocabulary
  - Decisions influenced by your work
  
  ### Building Path to Authority
  
  ```
  Stage 1: Practitioner
  - Do the work
  - Document learnings
  - Build track record
  
  Stage 2: Contributor
  - Share insights
  - Help others
  - Build reputation
  
  Stage 3: Recognized Expert
  - Others seek your input
  - Invited to opportunities
  - Referenced by peers
  
  Stage 4: Thought Leader
  - Shape industry conversation
  - Others follow your lead
  - Define the narrative
  ```
  
  ### What to Say Instead
  
  | Don't Say | Do Say |
  |-----------|--------|
  | "I'm a thought leader" | "I focus on X" |
  | "Leading expert" | "I've spent 10 years on X" |
  | "Guru" | "I help people with X" |
  | "Visionary" | "I believe X will happen" |
  
  Let your work speak. Let others anoint you.
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Self-describes as thought leader
  - Few external citations
  - Limited speaking invitations
### **Detection Pattern**
thought leader|industry expert|guru

## Platitude Content

### **Id**
platitude-content
### **Summary**
Publishing generic advice
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
Content is forgettable and interchangeable
### **Why**
  Generic content has no value.
  Anyone could have said it.
  Wastes audience attention.
  
### **Solution**
  ## Creating Non-Generic Content
  
  ### The Substitution Test
  
  ```
  If you could put anyone else's name on this content
  and it would still work, it's not thought leadership.
  
  Good: "When I was at [company], we discovered..."
  Bad: "Communication is important for teams"
  ```
  
  ### Non-Generic Content Types
  
  **Experience-Based**
  - What you learned from failure
  - What surprised you
  - What you'd do differently
  
  **Contrarian**
  - What you disagree with
  - Why common advice is wrong
  - Unpopular but true
  
  **Novel Framework**
  - New way to think about problem
  - Named methodology
  - Unique synthesis
  
  **Specific Data**
  - Your research
  - Your numbers
  - Your observations
  
  ### Platitude Detection
  
  | Platitude | Add Specificity |
  |-----------|-----------------|
  | "Hire great people" | "Here's my exact interview process" |
  | "Focus on customers" | "We talk to 5 customers/week using..." |
  | "Move fast" | "We ship daily because of this process" |
  | "Culture matters" | "We fired our top performer because..." |
  
  ### The "So What" Test
  
  After every statement, ask "So what?"
  If you can't answer with specific implication, cut it.
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Content could be said by anyone
  - Low engagement
  - Nothing memorable
### **Detection Pattern**
generic|platitude|obvious

## Expertise Without Humility

### **Id**
expertise-without-humility
### **Summary**
Never admitting uncertainty
### **Severity**
medium
### **Situation**
Appears arrogant or out of touch
### **Why**
  Nobody knows everything.
  False confidence erodes trust.
  Leaves no room to learn publicly.
  
### **Solution**
  ## Confident Humility
  
  ### Balance Formula
  
  ```
  Strong opinions + Openness to new data
  
  "Based on my experience, I believe X.
   Here's my evidence.
   I could be wrong if Y."
  ```
  
  ### Signaling Expertise + Humility
  
  **Claim Expertise On**
  - What you've done
  - Data you've seen
  - Decisions you've made
  
  **Acknowledge Limits On**
  - What's outside experience
  - Where data is limited
  - Rapidly changing areas
  
  ### Humility Phrases
  
  | Arrogant | Humble but Confident |
  |----------|----------------------|
  | "The answer is X" | "In my experience, X" |
  | "You should always" | "What's worked for me" |
  | "Wrong approach" | "I'd consider instead" |
  | "Obviously" | "What I've found" |
  
  ### Learning in Public
  
  ```
  Share:
  - What you got wrong
  - How your views evolved
  - What you're still figuring out
  
  This builds more trust than perfection.
  ```
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Seen as arrogant
  - Pushback in comments
  - No nuance in positions
### **Detection Pattern**
always|never|obviously|everyone knows

## Platform Burnout

### **Id**
platform-burnout
### **Summary**
Unsustainable content pace
### **Severity**
medium
### **Situation**
Exhausted by content creation
### **Why**
  Started too ambitiously.
  Not sustainable long-term.
  Quality suffers.
  
### **Solution**
  ## Sustainable Thought Leadership
  
  ### Pace Calculation
  
  ```
  Sustainable pace = (Hours available / Hours per piece)
  
  Example:
  - 4 hours/week for content
  - 2 hours per short piece
  - Sustainable: 2 pieces/week
  
  Don't plan for more.
  ```
  
  ### Sustainability Strategies
  
  **1. Batch Creation**
  - Dedicate blocks of time
  - Create multiple pieces at once
  - Reduces context switching
  
  **2. Repurposing**
  - 1 long piece → multiple short pieces
  - Same idea, different formats
  - Leverage existing work
  
  **3. Curation**
  - Share others' work with commentary
  - Less creation, still value
  - Builds relationships
  
  **4. Collaboration**
  - Guest posts
  - Interviews
  - Co-creation
  
  ### Burnout Prevention
  
  | Warning Sign | Response |
  |--------------|----------|
  | Dreading creation | Reduce frequency |
  | Quality declining | Take a break |
  | No new ideas | Consume more, create less |
  | Feeling performative | Reconnect with why |
  
  ### Minimum Viable Consistency
  
  ```
  Better than stopping:
  - Reduce frequency
  - Shorter content
  - Curation mode
  - Guest content
  
  Consistency > volume
  ```
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Falling behind on content
  - Quality declining
  - Resentment toward creating
### **Detection Pattern**
burnout|exhausted|can't keep up

## Audience Of Peers Only

### **Id**
audience-of-peers-only
### **Summary**
Only reaching other thought leaders
### **Severity**
medium
### **Situation**
Content gets likes from industry peers, not buyers
### **Why**
  Peers don't buy your product.
  Echo chamber limits reach.
  Misses actual target audience.
  
### **Solution**
  ## Reaching Beyond Peers
  
  ### Audience Analysis
  
  ```
  Who engages with your content?
  - Other thought leaders?
  - Industry observers?
  - Actual practitioners?
  - Buyers/decision makers?
  
  Goal: Reach practitioners and buyers,
        not just peers.
  ```
  
  ### Content Adjustments
  
  | Peer-Focused | Practitioner-Focused |
  |--------------|----------------------|
  | Meta-commentary | How-to content |
  | Industry hot takes | Problem-solving |
  | Abstract frameworks | Concrete examples |
  | Strategic vision | Tactical steps |
  
  ### Distribution Channels
  
  **Peer Channels**
  - Industry publications
  - Thought leader Slack groups
  - VC Twitter
  
  **Practitioner Channels**
  - Community forums
  - Trade publications
  - Practical podcasts
  - Search/SEO content
  
  ### Validation Metrics
  
  ```
  Vanity: Likes from big names
  Real: Comments from practitioners
         DMs from potential customers
         Business inquiries
         Practitioner citations
  ```
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Only peers engage
  - No business from content
  - Echo chamber engagement
### **Detection Pattern**
audience|engagement|reach
